The Conversation

Conducted [Feburary 24, 2026] ¬∑ [Format: Video Call] ¬∑ Approx. [55] minutes

You
So just to start, does agentic AI actually have too much control right now in your opinion?
Them
Yeah. In a lot of deployments, honestly, yes. And I say that as someone who's building this stuff. The systems we're creating can make hundreds of decisions an hour, financial decisions, operational decisions, usually with very little human involvement. That's the whole pitch. But just because we can remove humans from the loop doesn't mean we always should. I think the industry moved faster than the safeguards did.
You
What's an example of it going wrong because of that?
Them
I can't name clients obviously, but we've seen situations where an agent is given a goal, like clearing a backlog of invoices, and it just does it, pretty efficently too. But some of those invoices had flags on them that a human would have noticed. The agent didn't care, it had a goal and it hit the goal. Everything was technically correct and the outcome was still a mess. That's the thing people don't get, these systems don't second-guess themselves, they just do it and think they're in the right.
You
Right now its a lot of businesses using it for efficency, but regular people are starting to use agentic AI too. Like AI that books appointments, answers emails, manages files. Is that just as risky?
Them
It's a different kind of risky. In enterprise there's at least theoretically someone responsible, like a compliance team or an IT department. When it's just a person who downloaded an app and said "sure, manage my calendar and inbox," there's no one to fix or manage that. And these tools ask for a lot of permissions upfront. Access to your email, your contacts, files. Most people click through that without really thinking about what they just handed over. Obviously the agent needs those permissions to do what it does, but it also means if something goes wrong, or if the company behind it has bad data practices, your information is very exposed.
You
What kind of data are we actually talking about? Like what does it see?
Them
More than people realize. Like if an agent is managing your email it's reading everything, not just the thread you pointed it to. It needs context. So it might be seeing your medical appointment confirmations, your financial statements coming in, personal conversations. Same with a scheduling agent that has calendar access. It can infer a lot about your life just from patterns. Where you are, who you meet with, what you do on weekends. None of that feels like "data" when you're just trying to get help scheduling a dentist appointment. But it is data, and it's being processed somewhere.
You
Do you think most people using these tools day to day even know this stuff?
Them
No. And I don't say that to be condescending, I say it because the products are deliberately designed to feel simple and trustworthy. The whole philosophy is "just tell it what you want." The ease behind it is the product, there's no challenge. But we really forget challenge sometimes exists for a reason. When you had to manually do something, you were also manually reviewing it, you know your own actions best and the context behind them. Those moments where you actually do things in your daily lives are oversight which AI removes. And for a lot of tasks that's fine, great even. But people should at least know that the oversight is gone, not just assume someone else is handling it.
About the Interview
Why Ask These Questions?

The focus of this interview was to shed light on what people don't know about really how MUCH control Agentic AI has in our lives, especially as its becoming a bigger and bigger trend. My questions focused a lot on how agentic AI works with most people's day to day lives and what's really going on under the hood as a result.
